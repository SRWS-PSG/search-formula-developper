リサーチクエッション 
"How do the goals, content, delivery methods, and evaluation of faculty　development programs for clinicians differ across medical specialties?" 

 

キーワード 
Faculty development, Specialty-specific teaching practices, Clinical educator roles, Program design in medical education, Professional identity formation 


方法 
This scoping review protocol has been developed in accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR) guidelines. 

To guide the conduct of this review, we will follow the five-stage methodological framework proposed by the Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI) for scoping reviews. 

These five stages are as follows: 
 (1) identifying the research question; 
 (2) identifying relevant studies; 
 (3) study selection; 
 (4) charting the data; and 
 (5) collating, summarizing, and reporting the results. 

 Each of these stages is described in detail in the following sections. 

4.1. Research question 
This scoping review aims to address the following research question:  
How do the goals, content, delivery methods, and evaluation of FD programs for clinicians differ across medical specialties? 

4.2.  Identifying Relevant Studies (Eligibility Criteria) 

The inclusion criteria for this scoping review were defined using the Population–Concept–Context (PCC) framework, as recommended by the Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI Manual for Evidence Synthesis, Chapter 11: Scoping Reviews. 2020. Available from: https://synthesismanual.jbi.global). 

4.2.1 Participants 
This review will include studies involving clinicians who are physicians engaged in educational activities in clinical settings. Eligible participants are licensed physicians who provide teaching or supervision to medical students, residents, or fellows as part of undergraduate or postgraduate medical education. 

Clinical settings may include university hospitals, general hospitals, and outpatient clinics. 
Studies focusing exclusively on non-physician health professionals (e.g., nurses, pharmacists, therapists) will be excluded, unless data specific to physicians can be clearly extracted. 

4.2.2 Concept 

Eligible studies must report on faculty development (FD) programs implemented for physician educators involved in clinical education. The core concept of this review is FD programs that aim to enhance the educational competencies of clinicians who serve as educators (1,2,8). These competencies may include teaching skills, instructional design, assessment strategies, and other relevant abilities that support the clinician’s role in medical education (9). 

Studies focusing exclusively on pre-clinical education (e.g., basic science instruction unrelated to clinical practice) or programs designed for non-clinical teaching roles will be excluded. Programs focused solely on continuing professional development (CPD) for clinical knowledge or technical procedures—without an explicit educational component—will also be excluded. 

Programs that address both clinical and educational components will be included if the educational component is clearly described and relevant to faculty development. 

 

Eligible studies must describe at least one of the following aspects of an FD program: 

the goals of the program, 

its content, 

delivery methods, or 

evaluation approaches. 

FD programs may take various forms, including but not limited to workshops, seminars, longitudinal programs, peer learning, mentorship, or online modules (1,2,10). 

FD programs are diverse in format and focus, but they commonly aim to enhance both core teaching skills and broader professional competencies relevant to clinical educators (1,3,8). These elements reflect a holistic approach to educator development, addressing not only instructional techniques but also the leadership and interpersonal skills necessary for effective teaching in complex clinical environments (8,9). 

4.2.3 Context 
This review will include studies on faculty development (FD) programs for physician educators conducted in any clinical education setting, across all medical specialties and countries. Clinical education settings are broadly defined to include environments where clinicians are trained—such as hospitals, outpatient clinics, academic centers, community practices, simulation labs, and classrooms—provided the FD program targets clinicians in their educational role (1,2). 

Studies will be eligible if they address any of the FD components noted above, including goals, content, delivery methods, or evaluation. Programs will be excluded if they focus solely on pre-clinical instruction or target only non-clinician educators. 

No limits will be placed on publication date or geographic location. Studies published in languages other than English will be included if an English abstract is available. 

This broad inclusion of settings and specialties reflects the recognition that educational roles of clinical faculty vary across contexts, and FD programs must be adaptable to these diverse needs (1,9).
