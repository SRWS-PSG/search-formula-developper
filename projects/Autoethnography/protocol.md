We are interested in autoethnographic approaches conducted by research groups that include medical students, physicians, and medical education scholars. The research questions are as follows:
What are autoethnography, collaborative or collective autoethnography, and other practices described as autoethnographic approaches?
What significance or value has been attributed to the use of autoethnographic approaches by medical students, physicians, and medical education scholars?
What examples of research exist in which autoethnographic approaches are conducted by research groups that include medical students, physicians, and medical education scholars?
What ethical considerations have been addressed in such studies?

Keywords
　　autoethnography; collaborative autoethnography; medical education; narrative writing; reflective writing

4. Methods
This protocol is reported in accordance with the PRISMA Extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA ScR). The scoping review will be conducted using the five stage framework proposed by the Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI).

4.1 Stage 1: Identifying the research questions
The research questions have been specified in Section 2 above.

4.2 Stage 2: Identifying relevant studies (eligibility criteria)
The eligibility criteria were defined using the Population, Concept, and Context (PCC) framework.

4.2.1 Participants
Medical students, physicians, and medical education scholars.

4.2.2 Concept
Autoethnography, collaborative or collective autoethnography, and practices described as autoethnographic approaches.
Exclusion criteria:
Studies focusing on narrative writing or reflective writing that are not explicitly described as autoethnographic approaches, as well as autoethnographies conducted by patients.

4.2.3 Context
Studies conducted by research groups that include medical students, physicians, and medical education scholars, regardless of purpose, such as education, reflection, or interpretation of personal patient experiences.
