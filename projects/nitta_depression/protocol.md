**Abstract**

 **Objective** :
The goal is to systematically map and characterize the existing observational
literature evaluating depression following a cancer diagnosis using Japanese
real-world data (RWD). The focus will be on describing the data sources,
operational definitions of depression, and analytical approaches employed
across studies.[]()[.]()[[YK1]](#_msocom_1) [[新田　幸絵2]](#_msocom_2)

 **Introduction** :
Depression is a common and clinically relevant outcome among cancer patients
and has been examined in relation to quality of life, healthcare utilization,
and survival. Japan has cultivated a unique, multi-layered RWD environment
derived from routinely collected healthcare and registry data, enabling
large-scale observational research in oncology and mental health. While several
Japanese RWD-based studies have examined depression and related mental health
outcomes in cancer patients, the selection and reporting of methodological choices—such
as outcome definitions, follow-up structures, and analytical approaches to
common RWD-related challenges—has not been systematically mapped across studies.

[]()[]()[**Methods:**]()**[[YK3]](#_msocom_3) ****[[YK4]](#_msocom_4) ****[[新田　幸絵5]](#_msocom_5) **
The
methodological approach of this scoping review is guided by the JBI framework,
and reporting will follow the PRISMA-ScR guidelines. Searches will be conducted
in PubMed, Web of Science, and Ichushi Web from database inception. Study
selection and data extraction will be performed independently by two authors
using a standardized extraction form. The data will be synthesized
descriptively to map database characteristics, operational definitions of
depression, and analytical approaches used to address common challenges in
real-world data.

 **Inclusion
criteria** : This scoping review will include
observational studies of patients diagnosed with cancer (Population) that
evaluate depression as an outcome using individual-level real-world data
(Concept). Eligible studies will be conducted within the Japanese healthcare
context and will utilize routinely collected administrative or clinical
databases (Context). Depression must be operationally defined using diagnostic
codes, prescription records, or algorithm-based definitions. Studies based
solely on screening instruments will be excluded. If anxiety disorders or
suicide-related outcomes are evaluated alongside depression within the same
analytical framework, these outcomes will be included as secondary outcomes.

 **Keywords** :
depression; cancer; real-world data; Japan; scoping review; observational
studies; methodological approaches

# **1. **Introduction

**1.1
Background**

Japan's
universal health insurance system generates extensive healthcare utilization
data that can be leveraged for real-world evidence (RWE) research. Key
national-level data sources include the National Database of Health Insurance
Claims and Specific Health Checkups (NDB)[1], commercial
claims databases (e.g. JMDC, Inc. (JMDC) Claims Database[2]), the DPC
database[3], and the
National Cancer Registry of Japan[4]. These
datasets allow large-scale, population-based observational studies in
real-world clinical settings.

Depression is frequently reported among
patients with cancer [5] and has
been examined in relation to quality of life[6,7], healthcare
utilization[8], and survival
outcomes[9] in
previous studies. Evaluating depression and related mental health outcomes
using RWD has therefore become a research priority.

RWE research has evolved globally, with
various regions developing distinct methodologies adapted to their specific
healthcare infrastructures and database architectures[10,11]. In
parallel with international developments in RWD-based mental health research [12-14], Japan
has cultivated a unique and multi-layered RWD environment, including the NDB,
JMDC, DPC data, and cancer registries. Several observational studies using
administrative claims data, DPC data, and registry-based datasets have examined
depression or related mental health outcomes in cancer populations. However,
how methodological choices—such as outcome definitions, follow-up structures,
and analytical approaches to common RWD-related challenges—are selected and
reported across studies has not been systematically mapped. [In line]()[[新田　幸絵6]](#_msocom_6)  with these
concerns, prior systematic reviews conducted in Western settings have
documented substantial methodological heterogeneity in administrative
data–based research on mental health outcomes among cancer populations,
including variability in depression definitions, follow-up structures, and
analytical approaches, thereby limiting cross-study comparability and raising
concerns regarding outcome misclassification[15-17].

A growing number of observational studies
have used Japanese RWD to examine depression and related mental health outcomes
in cancer populations. However, it is unclear to what extent these studies
address the methodological issues repeatedly highlighted in prior international
reviews, such as outcome misclassification, lack of validation, and
design-specific analytical limitations. Accordingly, this scoping review aims
to map and characterize key methodological features of Japanese RWD-based
studies on depression following cancer diagnosis, with a particular focus on
the following aspects:

•
Variability in operational definitions: Operational
definitions of depression vary across Japanese RWD-based studies, with
definitions based on diagnostic codes (e.g., ICD-10), prescription records for
antidepressants, or study-specific algorithms. However, how these definitions
are operationalized and whether validation against external or clinical
reference standards is reported has not been systematically mapped.[]()[.]()[[YK7]](#_msocom_7) [[新田　幸絵8]](#_msocom_8)

•
Methodological challenges common to RWD
research—such as competing risks of death, time-dependent exposure definitions,
and missing data—are addressed using various analytical strategies, depending
on data structure and study objectives.[]()[.]()[[YK9]](#_msocom_9) [[新田　幸絵10]](#_msocom_10)

•
Existing studies appear to differ in terms
of data sources, study designs, target populations—including age groups and
clinical contexts—outcome definitions, and analytical strategies. At present,
there is no integrated overview that maps how different patient populations are
represented across studies or clarifies how key design and analytical decisions
are distributed across the literature.

[In]()[[新田　幸絵11]](#_msocom_11)
this review, depression is treated as the primary outcome; anxiety disorders
and suicide-related outcomes are included as secondary outcomes only when they
are evaluated alongside depression. This allows us to capture studies that
assess multiple related mental health outcomes within a single RWD-based
analytical framework.

**1.2 Why a
Scoping Review is Appropriate**

The
aim of this scoping review is not to assess reporting quality or to conduct
formal risk-of-bias evaluations of individual studies. Rather, the review seeks
to describe and map the current methodological landscape of Japanese RWD-based
research on depression following cancer diagnosis. Previous reviews have shown
that heterogeneity in outcome definitions and analytical
strategies—particularly in studies based on routinely collected administrative
data—often precludes quantitative synthesis or formal quality appraisal;
therefore, mapping how methodological choices are distributed across studies
represents a more appropriate approach than judging individual effect estimates..

**1.3 Preliminary
Search and Evidence Availability**

A
preliminary search of MEDLINE, the Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews, and
JBI Evidence Synthesis was conducted in January 2026. No systematic or scoping
reviews were identified that comprehensively address the methods and evidence
of depression research in Japanese cancer populations using RWD.

**1.4 Review
Objective**

The
objective of this scoping review is to map and characterize observational
studies that evaluate depression as an outcome after cancer diagnosis using
Japanese RWD. In accordance with the JBI framework, this review is structured
around the following elements: cancer patients of all ages (Population),
methodological and analytical approaches used to evaluate depression as an
outcome (Concept), and observational research conducted within the Japanese RWD
(Context). Specifically, this review aims to:

1. Identify
   the types and characteristics of Japanese RWD sources used in studies
   evaluating depression after cancer diagnosis (e.g. NDB, JMDC, DPC,
   registry-based or linked datasets).
2. Describe
   how depression is operationally defined as an outcome across studies, including
   the use of diagnostic codes, prescription records, or algorithms, and whether
   validation against clinical standards is reported.
3. Describe
   which outcome measures related to depression (e.g., incidence, prevalence,
   mortality, healthcare utilization) have been reported, and how they are
   operationalized.

[]()[4.
Map the statistical approaches used
in longitudinal observational studies—particularly cohort studies—to address
methodological challenges relevant to depression as an outcome, such as
competing risks of death and time-dependent biases.]()[[YK12]](#_msocom_12) [[新田　幸絵13]](#_msocom_13)

5. Map
   the distribution of study characteristics across patient populations (e.g.
   pediatric, AYA, elderly) and over time, in order to identify under-represented
   populations, data sources, and research areas.

# **2. **Review Questions

**2.1 Primary
Review Question**

What
are the characteristics of the data sources, operational definitions of
depression, and methodological approaches employed in observational studies
utilizing Japanese RWD to evaluate depression following cancer diagnosis?

**2.2 Secondary
Review Questions**

1. Data
   Source Utilization: Which Japanese RWD sources (e.g. NDB, JMDC, DPC, National
   Cancer Registry) have been utilized, and what are their specific
   characteristics and temporal trends in use?
2. Operational
   Definitions and Validation: How is depression operationally defined (e.g.
   ICD-10 codes, antidepressant prescriptions, or custom algorithms), and to what
   extent have these definitions been validated against clinical standards?
3. Outcomes
   and Temporal Trends: What clinical and healthcare outcomes (e.g. incidence,
   prevalence, mortality, healthcare utilization) are reported for depression
   following cancer [diagnosis]()[[新田　幸絵14]](#_msocom_14)  (and, where
   assessed alongside depression, related secondary mental health outcomes), and
   how do these outcomes vary across different follow-up durations?
4. Methodological
   Approaches: How are complex methodological challenges, such as competing risks
   of death, time-dependent biases, and missing data, addressed in the analytical
   models?
5. Evidence
   Gaps: Based on the mapped evidence, which data sources or patient populations
   (e.g. elderly or AYA patients) appear to be less frequently represented in the
   existing literature?

# **3. **Methods

**3.1
Protocol and reporting framework**

The
proposed scoping review will be conducted in accordance with the JBI
methodology for scoping reviews[18]. This
protocol has been developed in adherence to the Preferred Reporting Items for
Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR)
checklist[19,20]. The
protocol is registered on the Open Science Framework (OSF) (https://osf.io/).

# **3.2 Eligibility Cr[]()[iteria]()** **[[YK15]](#_msocom_15) ****[[新田　幸絵16]](#_msocom_16) **

**3.2.1
Participants**

This
review includes cancer patients of all ages following cancer diagnosis, and including
all types of cancer (solid tumors and hematologic malignancies), as well as all
disease stages (Stage I to Stage IV, from localized to metastatic). The review
includes newly diagnosed cases, recurrences, and secondary cancers, with no
restrictions based on treatment modality or status. Studies focusing solely on
populations without cancer are excluded.

**3.2.2
Concept**

The
primary concept of this review is the methodological and analytical approaches
used to evaluate depression as a primary outcome using Japanese RWD. This
includes the examination of how depression is operationally defined and
statistically analyzed within the Japanese healthcare context. Where studies
additionally evaluate anxiety disorders or suicide-related outcomes alongside
depression, the operational definitions and analytical approaches for these
secondary outcomes will also be examined. Anxiety disorders and suicide-related
outcomes are considered secondary outcomes and will be included only when they
are evaluated alongside depression within the same RWD-based studies or
analytical frameworks; studies that assess these outcomes without evaluating
depression will be excluded. Specifically, eligible studies must define
depression as an outcome using at least one of the following operational
definitions: diagnostic codes (e.g. ICD-10 F32–F34), prescription records (e.g.
antidepressants, ATC code N06A), or validated algorithms combining both codes
and prescriptions. If anxiety disorders or suicide-related outcomes are
additionally assessed within the same study, these outcomes must also be
defined using comparable code- or prescription-based operational definitions.
For suicide-related outcomes, this review will not impose a single standardized
operational definition; instead, how such outcomes are defined and reported
within individual RWD-based studies (e.g. diagnostic codes, self-harm–related
codes, or registry-based mortality outcomes) will be descriptively recorded
when assessed alongside depression.
Studies will be included if they report on incidence,
prevalence, or risk factors where these mental health outcomes are evaluated
through the prism of RWD-specific methodologies. This review focuses on the
methodological rigor and diversity of these approaches, including how studies
address specific RWD challenges such as competing risks of death,
time-dependent biases, and the handling of missing data. Consequently, studies
defining outcomes solely based on screening tool scores (e.g. PHQ-9, HADS) are
excluded, as they do not employ the routine clinical data structures that are
the focus of this methodological mapping.

**3.2.3
Context**

This
review will focus on the Japanese RWD environment. Following the classification
system established by Yamana et al.[21], eligible
studies will include those utilizing the following types of administrative and
clinical databases: (1) health insurance claims data (e.g. NDB, commercial
databases such as JMDC, and municipal insurance claims); (2) DPC data (e.g. DPC
survey data and MID-NET®); (3) []()[Electronic Health Record (EHR)
data]()[[YK17]](#_msocom_17) [[新田　幸絵18]](#_msocom_18) ; (4) disease
registry data (e.g. national or hospital-based cancer registries); and (5)
linked datasets involving individual-level linkage between these sources, such
as the NDB linked with the National Cancer Registry. In line with the focus on
routinely collected Japanese real-world data, studies based solely on
single-facility observational designs or unstructured chart reviews will be
excluded unless they utilize a maintained, multi-institutional database
infrastructure.

To ensure the focus remains on routine
clinical practice, this review specifically includes studies utilizing
individual-level RWD that are routinely collected in Japan for clinical care,
insurance reimbursement, or administrative purposes. Conversely, we will
exclude datasets collected primarily for specific research purposes (e.g. ad
hoc surveys), studies limited to individual chart reviews without broader
database utilization, and analyses of aggregated data that lack
individual-level tracking. Studies conducted outside the Japanese healthcare
context are also excluded.

**3.2.4
Types of Sources**

This
scoping review will include observational study utilizing Japanese RWD. Eligible
study designs will encompass analytical observational studies including prospective
and retrospective cohort studies, case-control studies, and nested case-control
studies, as well as descriptive observational studies such as cross-sectional
studies and incidence/prevalence studies. In addition, validation studies evaluating
code-based or algorithm-based definitions of depression using Japanese RWD will
be included[]()[.]()[[YK19]](#_msocom_19) [[新田　幸絵20]](#_msocom_20)

**3.3 Search
strategy**

A
three-step search strategy will be utilized to locate both published and
unpublished studies.

1. Initial
   search: A limited search of PubMed (via NCBI) and Ichushi Web was conducted to
   identify relevant articles. The text words in titles and abstracts, along with
   the index terms (e.g. MeSH, Ichushi thesaurus), were analyzed to develop a
   comprehensive search strategy.
2. Database
   search: The full search strategy, incorporating identified keywords and index
   terms, will be adapted for each database, including PubMed (via NCBI), Web of
   Science (via Clarivate), and Ichushi Web (via Japan Medical Abstracts Society).
   The selection of Japanese healthcare databases included in the search strategy
   was based on the inventory of databases compiled by the Medical and Healthcare
   Database Utilization Committee of the Japanese Society for Pharmacoepidemiology[22]. The
   detailed search strategies for these databases are provided in Appendices 1, 2,
   and 3.
3. Supplemental
   search: The reference lists of all included evidence sources and relevant
   systematic reviews will be screened for additional studies. Gray literature
   will be considered only when sufficient methodological details are available to
   determine eligibility; conference abstracts or unpublished reports lacking
   adequate information on data sources or study design will not be included.

To
maximize sensitivity, the cancer concept was intentionally broad, incorporating
general neoplasm terms and commonly used cancer-related words. To minimize the
risk of overlooking relevant RWD-based studies, the search strategy
incorporates both database-specific names and generic RWD terminology. This
acknowledges the heterogeneity in how Japanese RWD sources are described across
publications. However, the eligibility assessment will apply Concept criteria
such that only studies evaluating depression as the primary outcome will be
included; those assessing anxiety disorders or suicide-related outcomes without
evaluating depression will be excluded; studies assessing anxiety disorders or
suicide-related outcomes without evaluating depression will be excluded.

**3.4 Study/Source
of evidence selection **

Following
the search, all identified citations will be collated and uploaded into EndNote
21 (Clarivate Analytics, PA, USA), and duplicates will be removed. The
screening process will be supported by Rayyan or ASReview to enhance efficiency
and consistency during the selection stage. Following a pilot test, titles and
abstracts will then be screened by two or more independent reviewers (Y.N. and
K.K.) against the inclusion criteria in two stages:

1. Title
   and Abstract Screening: Titles and abstracts will be independently assessed
   against the eligibility criteria. Any sources deemed "potentially
   eligible" by either reviewer will proceed to full-text review.
2. Full-text
   Review: The full text of selected citations will be assessed in detail by the
   two independent reviewers (Y.N. and K.K.) to determine final eligibility.
   Reasons for the exclusion of sources at the full-text stage will be recorded
   and reported in the scoping review. Any disagreements during the selection
   process will be resolved through discussion or consultation with a third
   reviewer (Y.K.). The results of the search and the study inclusion process will
   be reported in full and presented in a Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic
   Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA) 2020 flow
   diagram.

**3.5 Data
ext[]()[]()[]()[]()[raction]()**[[YK21]](#_msocom_21) [[新田　幸絵22]](#_msocom_22) [[YK23]](#_msocom_23) [[新田　幸絵24]](#_msocom_24) [[YK25]](#_msocom_25)

Data
will be extracted from all evidence sources included in the scoping review by
two reviewers (Y.N. and K.K.). A standardized data extraction form, developed
by the research team in accordance with the objectives of this review, will be used
to ensure consistency and accuracy in data collection. Prior to full data
extraction, the draft extraction form will be pilot-tested on 10 randomly
selected studies, and  refined as
necessary to improve clarity and completeness. Any discrepancies between
reviewers during the data extraction process will be resolved through
discussion and consensus. If consensus cannot be reached, a third member of the
research team (Y.K.) will be consulted. The artificial intelligence (AI) tool
is used only to supplement manual screening[23]. Human
reviewers develop and peer-review core search strategies, ensuring
methodological rigor independent of AI assistance. When relevant information is
unclear or not reported in the original publications, the data will be recorded
as not reported, and no attempts will be made to contact study authors for
additional information, consistent with the descriptive and mapping objectives
of this scoping review. Data extraction will focus on describing study
characteristics, methodological approaches, and outcome definitions, rather
than on assessing the methodological quality or risk of bias of individual
studies. Minor refinements to the data extraction tool may be made during the
extraction process, if necessary. Any such modifications will be transparently
reported in the final scoping review.

The
following items will be extracted:

•
 **Study identification and design** :
Author(s), year of publication, study period, study design (cross-sectional, cohort, case–control, nested case–control, or
validation study)

•
 **Database characteristics** :
Type of database source (administrative claims, DPC data, cancer registry, or
linked datasets), name of database used (e.g. NDB, JMDC), and data collection
period covered by the database

•
 **Participant characteristics** :
Cancer type, broad clinical context at the time of assessment (curative vs.
non-curative [advanced or recurrent] disease, where reported)[]()[, ]()[[YK26]](#_msocom_26) [[新田　幸絵27]](#_msocom_27) age characteristics
as reported (mean/median age, age categories, or specific life-stage groups
such as pediatric, AYA, or elderly), sample size

•
**Outcome definition:**
Operational definitions of depression as an outcome (e.g. diagnostic codes,
prescription records, algorithm-based definitions, or combinations thereof), timing
of depression assessment in relation to cancer diagnosis, whether validation of
the depression definition against clinical or external standards is reported. Where
anxiety disorders or suicide-related outcomes are evaluated alongside
depression, their operational definitions and assessment timing will also be
extracted as secondary outcomes.

•
 **Key methodological features and
reported measures extracted (design-specific)** :

For
cross-sectional studies: Reported
prevalence estimates or point-in-time measures of depression[24]

For
longitudinal observational studies (particularly cohort studies): 1) Reported
incidence measures (e.g. cumulative incidence, incidence rates) and risk
estimates (e.g. risk ratios, rate ratios, hazard ratios)[25], 2) Statistical
approaches used to address time-to-event and methodological challenges, such as
competing risks of death[26],
time-dependent exposures [27], or
missing data [28]

This
design-specific extraction strategy is intended to map how methodological and
analytical approaches vary according to study design, rather than to assess the
validity or strength of individual findings[20].

**3.6 Data
analysis and presentation **

The
extracted data will be presented in graphical, diagrammatic, or tabular form to
provide a visual map of the evidence. Where findings are not suitable for
visualization, or to provide necessary context, these will be presented through
descriptive narrative summaries. This dual approach ensures that the
presentation of results directly responds to the primary and secondary research
questions. The narrative synthesis will involve a qualitative mapping of the
evidence, with a particular focus on the following dimensions:

[]()[•
Extent and characteristics: The overall
volume and trends of Japanese RWE research using various databases, stratified
by study design and over time.]()

•
Operational definitions and validity: The
current state of diversity and methodological approaches regarding diagnostic
codes (e.g. ICD-10) and algorithms for depression, including their validation
status against clinical standards, mapped across database types and study
designs.

•
Methodological challenges: The strategies
employed to address complex analytical issues, such as statistical modeling for
competing risks (e.g. death) and the handling of missing data, with particular
attention to variation across different study designs (e.g., cross-sectional
vs. cohort studies).

•
Evidence gaps: The identification of
under-represented cancer types, database sources, study designs,　and
underserved populations (e.g. pediatric, AYA, or elderly patients) where
empirical evidence remains insufficient.[[YK28]](#_msocom_28) [[新田　幸絵29]](#_msocom_29)
